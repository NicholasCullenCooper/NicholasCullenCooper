## 1. Brand Identity Brief

### Positioning Statement

Nicholas Cooper is a software engineer, designer, photographer, and writer. He builds tools, thinks about how things work, and publishes what he learns -- about software, about design, about whatever earns his attention. His public presence is not a portfolio or a content channel. It is a body of work: code, images, writing, and ideas that accumulate over time and represent a person honestly.

### Why "Wide" is Honest, Not Dilutive

The instinct to narrow a public identity to one discipline comes from corporate branding logic: pick a lane, own a niche, be findable. But that logic produces a dishonest representation of how most interesting people actually think and work.

The better model is the personal site as **commonplace book** -- a running record of what you are making, reading, watching, thinking about, and building. The coherence comes not from subject-matter discipline but from the quality of attention. A short film review that notices something specific sits naturally next to a technical post that notices something specific. The signal is: this person pays attention.

**The risk is not breadth. The risk is shallowness.** A post that says something real about a film is better for your public identity than a post that says something generic about Kubernetes. Breadth with depth works. Breadth without thought does not.

### Brand Traits

1. **Attentive** -- notices things carefully, whether in code, in a frame, in a film, or in an argument
2. **Rigorous** -- brings craft and care to whatever he works on, from a CLI tool's README to a photograph's composition
3. **Honest** -- publishes work at various stages, admits when things fail, changes his mind in public
4. **Wide-ranging** -- an engineer who also thinks about design, philosophy, film, photography, and how things look and feel
5. **Self-directed** -- builds, writes, and photographs for his own reasons, not for an audience strategy

### Tone and Voice Guidelines

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Write like you're explaining to a smart friend over coffee | Write like you're pitching a VC or impressing a professor |
| Say "I tried X, it didn't work because Y" | Say "excited to announce" |
| Use first person, active voice, specific details | Use passive corporate voice or academic hedging |
| Have opinions and state them plainly | Perform false humility or false confidence |
| Let different subjects coexist without justification | Apologize for writing about something non-technical |
| Be specific about tradeoffs and decisions | Be vague to sound impressive or broadly applicable |
| Write about film, design, philosophy when you have something to say | Force cross-domain metaphors ("5 things photography taught me about code") |

**Voice in one line:** First-person, specific, opinionated, honest, comfortable moving between technical and non-technical subjects.

### What You Are Known For

- An AI hype promoter. You use AI tools seriously and think about their tradeoffs.

### How to Frame Experiments, Failed Ideas, and AI-Assisted Work

**Experiments:**
> "I wanted to see if X would work. Here's what I built, what I learned, and whether I'd do it again."

Frame as investigation, not product launch. The value is in the learning, not the artifact.

**Failed ideas:**
> "This didn't work, and here's why that's interesting."

Never apologize for trying something. Never pretend it succeeded. The signal is in the analysis, not the outcome.

**AI-assisted / vibe-coded work:**
> "I used Claude to scaffold this, then spent most of my time on the architecture and edge cases."

State the tool usage plainly. Emphasize your judgment: what you kept, what you rewrote, where you steered. AI is a power tool; mentioning you used a drill does not diminish the cabinet.

**The key frame:** You are the person who decides what to build, evaluates whether it works, and writes up what happened. The tools you use along the way are implementation details worth mentioning, not confessions worth defending.

### Domain Identity vs. Display Name

- **nicholascullencooper.com** is the canonical, permanent, owned identity. It appears in URLs, Bluesky handles, and anywhere you want machine-readable permanence.
- **Nicholas Cooper** is the human-facing display name. It appears in bios, bylines, conversation, and anywhere a person reads your name.
- The middle name (Cullen) serves as a namespace disambiguator, not a branding element. You do not need to use it in conversation or display contexts.
- Think of it like a domain name vs. a business card: the domain is `nicholascullencooper.com`, but the card says Nicholas Cooper.

### How to Present Non-Engineering Interests

The old playbook treated photography, design, philosophy, and film as "broader interests" that needed careful containment. That framing was wrong. Here is the corrected model:

**These are not "broader interests." They are parts of your identity.** The website is the home of Nicholas Cooper the person, not Nicholas Cooper the software engineer. The subjects you write about should reflect what you actually think about, not what a hiring manager expects to see.

**The coherence comes from voice and quality, not subject matter.** A short, sharp film review written with the same specificity as a technical postmortem belongs on the same site. The reader learns: this person pays attention to things.

**The risk model is simple:**
- Writing something thoughtful about a film: builds credibility (shows range and taste)
- Writing something shallow about a film: damages credibility (shows poor judgment about what's worth saying)
- Not writing about non-technical subjects: leaves credibility neutral but makes the identity thinner

**Practical approach:**
- Non-technical writing lives in the same `/writing` stream as technical writing, not in a segregated section
- Photography gets its own section because it is a visual medium that benefits from dedicated presentation
- No subject needs permission or justification. If you have something worth saying, say it.

## 3. Website Strategy

### Recommendation: Personal Site as Commonplace Book

Not a landing page (too thin). Not a blog (too much pressure, too narrow). Not a portfolio (too static, too curated). Build a **personal site that functions as a commonplace book** -- a living, growing record of what you make, think, and notice.

The site should feel like walking into someone's studio: there are finished projects on the wall, sketches on the desk, books open to interesting pages, photographs drying, and notes pinned everywhere. It is clearly the workspace of one person. It is wide but not scattered, because the quality of attention is consistent.

### Information Architecture

```
nicholascullencooper.com/
|-- /                    Home (hero + recent activity across all streams)
|-- /projects            Project index (software, design, creative)
|-- /projects/[slug]     Individual project pages
|-- /writing             Writing index (all writing in one stream)
|-- /writing/[slug]      Individual piece
|-- /photography         Curated gallery
|-- /about               About page
|-- /now                 Optional: "what I'm working on / thinking about now"
|-- /feed.xml            RSS feed for writing
```

### Why This Structure

**One writing stream, not separate blogs for separate subjects.** `/writing` contains technical posts, experiment reports, film reviews, design notes, philosophical observations -- all in one chronological stream, differentiated by tags, not by URL. This is the critical structural decision.

Why:
- Splitting by subject (e.g., `/blog` for tech, `/reviews` for film) creates the impression that the non-technical writing needs its own container because it does not belong with the "real" work. That is the wrong signal.
- A single stream says: "These are all things I wrote. They sit together because they are all me."
- Tags and filtering let visitors find what they want without forcing the author to categorize before creating.
- It mirrors how a commonplace book actually works: thoughts in sequence, indexed by topic.

**Projects are separate from writing.** A project page is a living document about a thing you built or designed. A piece of writing is a point-in-time artifact. They link to each other but live separately. Projects can be software, design systems, photography series, or anything else that is a sustained creative effort.

**Photography gets its own section.** It is a visual medium. It benefits from dedicated, image-first presentation. A curated gallery + link to Instagram.

**/now is optional but valuable.** "What I'm working on and thinking about right now." Can include what you're building, reading, watching, photographing. Updated when things change. This is the most natural place for the wide-ranging identity to be visible at a glance.

### Recommended Stack

**Astro** with Markdown/MDX content.

Why Astro:
- Content-first static site generator built for exactly this use case
- Markdown/MDX content collections with frontmatter-based typing
- Ships zero JavaScript by default (fast, simple)
- Can add interactive islands if needed (React, Svelte, whatever)
- Excellent developer experience for someone who writes Go and TypeScript
- Strong RSS support
- Easy deployment to Vercel, Netlify, or Cloudflare Pages
- Not going anywhere -- stable, well-maintained, growing ecosystem

Why not other options:
- **Next.js**: Overkill for a content site. You do not need SSR, API routes, or React hydration for a personal site.
- **Hugo**: Fine but Go templates are unpleasant for anything beyond basic layouts.
- **11ty**: Solid but Astro has better DX and TypeScript support.
- **Plain HTML**: Admirable but you will never update it.
- **Notion/Substack/etc.**: You own a domain. Own the site too.

**Hosting:** Cloudflare Pages or Vercel. Both are free, fast, and handle custom domains trivially. Cloudflare Pages if you want to consolidate DNS and hosting in one place.

| **Tool/library review** | Honest assessment of something you used | Occasional | "Using Drizzle ORM for 3 months: what I think" |

### Cadence Recommendations

**Do not commit to a schedule.** Instead, commit to a minimum:
- At least 2 pieces per month across all categories (keeps the site alive)
- At least 1 project page per quarter (keeps the portfolio current)
- No maximum. If you have a productive week, publish five things. If you have a quiet month, publish nothing.

**The anti-pattern to avoid:** Going silent for 6 months, then feeling like your next post needs to justify the gap. It doesn't. Just post the next thing.

### "Is This Worth Posting?" Decision Rules

**Post it if:**
- You would explain it to a friend and they'd find it interesting
- You noticed something specific that you haven't seen articulated elsewhere
- You made a non-obvious decision and can articulate why
- Something failed in an instructive way
- You have a genuine opinion, not a hot take
- You watched/read/used something and your reaction was more specific than "it was good" or "it was bad"

**Don't post it if:**
- It is a well-known observation with many existing articulations
- You are posting to fill a content calendar, not because you have something to say
- The writing is vague -- you have a feeling but not a thought yet
- You are not comfortable with a thoughtful stranger reading it in three years
- It is a subtweet, rant, or reaction to a discourse cycle you won't remember in a month

### How to Publish During vs. After a Project

**During:** Short notes. "Working on X, ran into Y, trying Z." These are lab notebook entries. They are short, timestamped, and allowed to be wrong. Tag them clearly.

**After:** Project page + writeup post. The project page is the living document. The writeup post is the story of building it. The writeup can reference the in-progress notes.

### How to Talk About Failed Experiments

**Frame:** "The question was interesting. The approach was reasonable. Here's what I learned."

**Structure:**
1. What I was trying to do (the question)
2. What I built (the attempt)
3. What happened (the result)
4. Why it didn't work (the analysis)
5. What I'd try differently (the forward look)

**Tone:** Matter-of-fact. Not self-deprecating. Not defensive. People respect someone who can analyze a failure clearly. They do not respect someone who either hides failures or performs excessive humility about them.

### How to Talk About Rough Code

**Do not apologize.** Instead, set context:

- "This is a prototype -- the architecture is intentional, the error handling is not."
- "Weekend experiment. The interesting part is the approach, not the implementation."
- "This works but I wouldn't ship it as-is. Here's what I'd change for production."

### How to Frame AI-Assisted Work

**Just say it.** State what tools you used and what role they played.

Good examples:
- "Scaffolded with Claude, then spent the real time on the data model and edge cases."
- "Used Copilot for the boilerplate. The architecture is mine."
- "This was a vibe-code prototype to test the concept. I'd rewrite the core logic before shipping."

### Writing About Non-Technical Subjects

**No special rules needed.** The same standards apply:

- Be specific. "This film does X well" is better than "this film is good."
- Have a perspective. You are not writing a Wikipedia summary.
- Notice something. The value is in the observation no one else made.
- Keep it as long as the thought requires and no longer.

**The integration is natural, not forced.** You do not need transition sentences like "as a software engineer, I notice that films also..." Just write about the film. The reader knows you are an engineer because they are on your site. Let the subjects coexist without explanation.

**Bad:** "5 Design Principles I Learned From Kubrick"
**Good:** "Kubrick's use of symmetry in The Shining isn't about beauty -- it's about wrongness. When something is too symmetrical, it feels artificial. I think about this when I'm designing interfaces, too, but that's a different post."

## 6. Risk Management and Comfort Rules

### Emotional Comfort Rules

1. **The morning-after test.** Before publishing, ask: "Will I be comfortable with this existing tomorrow?" If no, save it as a draft and revisit in 48 hours.
2. **The permanent record rule.** Anything on your domain-linked identity is effectively permanent. Write as if a thoughtful stranger will read it in three years.
3. **No posting when frustrated.** Never publish a rant, a subtweet, or a "hot take" you haven't sat with for at least a day.
4. **Rough is fine. Raw is not.** Rough code with a clear explanation is publishable. Emotionally raw venting is not.
5. **You can always post later.** There is no urgency. If something feels uncomfortable, wait.

### Reputation Rules

1. **Never trash-talk employers, colleagues, or specific people by name.** Critique ideas, technologies, design choices, and films -- not people or organizations.
2. **Never post credentials, API keys, or proprietary information.** Audit repos before making them public.
3. **Never claim credit for work that is not yours.** Credit tools, libraries, collaborators, and AI assistance.
4. **Opinions are fine. Overconfidence is not.** "I prefer X over Y because Z" is good. "Anyone who uses Y is wrong" is bad.
5. **Strong opinions about art, design, and ideas are expected and welcome.** You can say a film is bad. You can say a design trend is lazy. Just be specific about why.

### Employer-Safe / Common-Sense Rules

1. **Never share proprietary code, architecture, or business information from current or past employers.**
2. **If your employer has a social media policy, follow it.** When in doubt, ask.
3. **Side projects should not conflict with your employment.**
4. **Non-technical writing (film reviews, philosophy, design opinions) is almost always employer-safe.** Having opinions about Tarkovsky does not create HR problems.
5. **Keep work and personal projects in separate repos, accounts, and machines** where practical.

### "Too Raw to Publish" Signals

- You are publishing to prove something to someone specific
- The writing has a defensive or angry tone
- You cannot articulate what a reader would learn or notice
- The code doesn't run and you cannot explain why
- You are including it to hit a posting target
- You feel anxious about a specific person seeing it

### "Interesting Enough to Publish Even If Imperfect" Signals

- You noticed something specific that you haven't seen anyone else articulate
- A friend said "you should write that up"
- The failure mode was surprising or non-obvious
- Your reaction to a film/book/tool is more specific than you expected
- You have a clear position and can articulate the tradeoffs
- Someone else thinking about the same question would benefit from your notes

### Privacy and Safety with a Real-Name Identity

1. **Do not publish your home address, phone number, or personal email on public profiles.** Use a dedicated public email (e.g., hello@nicholascullencooper.com).
2. **Be deliberate about location information.** City-level is fine. Neighborhood or street-level is not.
3. **Photography on Instagram should not include identifiable locations of your home or daily routine patterns.**
4. **Domain WHOIS should use privacy protection.** Most registrars offer this by default.
5. **Strong opinions about art and ideas are fine under your real name.** Strong opinions about specific people or political factions require more caution -- not because they're wrong, but because they attract the wrong kind of attention.
